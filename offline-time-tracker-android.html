01 · THE PREMISE

Time tracking should reduce uncertainty, not create admin work.

The useful question is rarely “How many hours was my phone active?” It is “Where did the time I meant to control actually go?” A manual timer gives each session a purpose: client work, study, exercise, reading, or whatever matters to you.

An offline time tracker stores this working record on the device and keeps the core timer available without a connection. That is especially useful when you want a simple personal log, work while travelling, or do not want to create an account before starting a timer.

Keep the goal modest: you do not need a forensic record of every minute. A consistent record of the few activities you care about is much more useful than a perfect system you abandon after three days.

02 · SETUP

Start with fewer categories than you think you need.

Too many categories turn every timer start into a classification problem. Begin with three to seven outcomes you genuinely review. You can split a category later when the difference starts affecting a decision.

STEP—01 Name outcomes

Use “Client work,” “Study,” or “Training,” not a long list of individual tasks.

STEP—02 Start before the work

Attach pressing Start to the ritual of opening the document, book, or project.

STEP—03 Add a short note

Record the deliverable or topic when that context will matter during review.

STEP—04 Repair honest mistakes

Edit forgotten stop times instead of throwing away the whole day’s record.

03 · FOCUS MODE

Use Pomodoro for starting; use an open timer for staying.

A Pomodoro timer is valuable when the hard part is beginning. A defined focus interval lowers the commitment: you only have to work until the next break. It also helps when a task is repetitive or mentally tiring.

An open timer is better when interruptions are unlikely and stopping after a fixed interval would break useful momentum. Developers, writers, designers, and craftspeople often need both modes on different days.

A simple decision rule

  • Resisting the task? Begin with one Pomodoro cycle.
  • Already in flow? Continue with the normal timer.
  • Need an invoice or study log? Track the complete session either way.

04 · REVIEW

Spend ten minutes turning history into a decision.

At the end of the week, compare intention with reality. Look for one activity receiving too little time, one receiving more than expected, and one recurring interruption worth changing. Daily, weekly, and monthly charts are useful only when they lead to a small adjustment.

  1. Check totals by activity rather than judging individual days.
  2. Correct obvious forgotten timers so they do not distort the pattern.
  3. Export a CSV when you need an invoice, archive, or deeper spreadsheet analysis.
  4. Choose one change for the next week—one, not seven.
